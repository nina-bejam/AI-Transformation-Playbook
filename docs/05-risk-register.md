## Why Risk Management Matters

One of the biggest differences between software development and project management is the way success is measured.

As a developer, I often focused on building the right solution.

As someone increasingly involved in project leadership and coordination, I have learned that successful delivery depends not only on implementation, but also on identifying risks early and reducing their impact before they become issues.

In my experience, most project problems do not appear suddenly.

They usually start as small risks that were either ignored, underestimated, or never discussed.

For that reason, I consider risk management one of the most important responsibilities of a Project Manager.

---

## Risk Assessment Approach

For each risk I evaluate:

* Probability of occurrence
* Potential business impact
* Mitigation strategy
* Risk owner
* Current status

The objective is not to eliminate all risks.

The objective is to understand them early enough to make informed decisions.

## Risks I Would Monitor Most Closely

If I were leading this initiative, the following risks would receive the highest attention:

### Stakeholder Misalignment

Many projects fail because different groups expect different outcomes.

Executive leadership, technical teams, and business users often define success differently.

Maintaining alignment throughout the project is critical.

### Unrealistic AI Expectations

AI is often expected to solve problems that are organizational rather than technical.

Clear communication about capabilities and limitations is essential.

### Change Resistance

Even successful technology can fail if users do not trust or adopt it.

Adoption should be treated as a project objective, not as an afterthought.

### Scope Expansion

Once stakeholders see early success, additional requirements often appear.

Without clear prioritization, timelines and budgets can quickly become unrealistic.

---

## Risk Review Process

I would review risks:

* Weekly during active project delivery
* Monthly with leadership stakeholders
* Before major project milestones
* Whenever significant scope, budget, or timeline changes occur

New risks should be documented as soon as they are identified.

---

## Escalation Criteria

Risks should be escalated when:

* Impact becomes High
* Probability increases significantly
* Delivery dates are threatened
* Budget limits are exceeded
* Security or compliance concerns emerge

The goal of escalation is not to assign blame.

The goal is to enable timely decision-making.

---

## Final Thoughts

From my perspective, risk management is not about avoiding risk.

Every meaningful project involves uncertainty.

The responsibility of a Project Manager is to make risks visible, communicate them clearly, and help stakeholders make informed decisions.

Projects rarely fail because risks exist.

Projects fail because risks are discovered too late.
